# Presentation Design Styles Reference

> 30 named styles synthesized from community design systems.
> Each style includes color palette, typography, layout principles, and usage guidance.
> Source: Adapted from corazzon/pptx-design-styles and astraeus design conventions.

## Style 01: Executive Suite

### Overview
Executive Suite communicates authority and gravitas through deep navy foundations and restrained gold accents. The palette evokes institutional trust — think boardrooms, investor briefings, and C-suite strategy decks. Every element earns its place; decoration is minimal, hierarchy is absolute.

### Color Palette
- Primary: `#1A3A5C` (Deep Navy)
- Secondary: `#2E6DA4` (Corporate Blue)
- Accent: `#C9A84C` (Antique Gold)
- Background: `#FFFFFF` (White)
- Text Primary: `#1A1A1A` (Near Black)
- Text Secondary: `#6B6B6B` (Medium Gray)
- Border: `#D8D8D8` (Light Gray)

### Typography
- Heading: Playfair Display, Bold, 36–44pt — conveys tradition and weight
- Body: Source Sans Pro, Regular, 16–18pt — readable at projector distance
- Caption: IBM Plex Mono, Regular, 10–11pt, wide letter-spacing — for labels and footnotes

### Layout Principles
- Left-aligned content with a strong left margin rule (4pt navy vertical bar)
- Title block occupies top 25% of slide; body content below
- Single horizontal divider between title and body in navy
- Maximum 4 bullet points per slide; prefer 2–3
- 48pt generous padding on all edges

### When to Use
- Board-level strategy and investor presentations
- Annual reports and performance reviews
- High-stakes client pitches where credibility is paramount
- Executive briefings requiring formal visual register

### Anti-Patterns
- Avoid gradients — flat blocks only preserve the formal aesthetic
- Never use more than two accent touches per slide; gold should be sparse
- Avoid rounded corners on shapes; use sharp rectangles
- No decorative illustrations or stock photography backgrounds

## Style 02: Corporate Blue

### Overview
Corporate Blue is the trusted workhorse of business presentations — professional, broadly legible, and safe across industries. The mid-range blue palette reads as competent and forward-looking without the intimidation of deep navy. Designed for regular stakeholder communication where clarity outranks personality.

### Color Palette
- Primary: `#0057A8` (Corporate Blue)
- Secondary: `#0083CA` (Sky Blue)
- Accent: `#F5A623` (Amber)
- Background: `#F8FAFC` (Off-White)
- Text Primary: `#212B36` (Dark Slate)
- Text Secondary: `#637381` (Slate Gray)
- Border: `#DFE3E8` (Cool Gray)

### Typography
- Heading: Montserrat, SemiBold, 32–40pt — clean and recognizable
- Body: Open Sans, Regular, 15–17pt — legible and neutral
- Caption: Open Sans, Light, 10pt, uppercase — for metadata and labels

### Layout Principles
- Full-width header bar in primary blue containing slide title in white
- Content area uses 2-column grid for data-heavy slides
- Accent color limited to call-out boxes and key statistics
- Slide numbers bottom-right, consistent every slide
- Visual weight descends: title > key stat > body > caption

### When to Use
- Regular internal business presentations and team updates
- Cross-department proposals where visual neutrality is needed
- Client-facing status reports and project dashboards
- Industry conference talks aimed at mixed professional audiences

### Anti-Patterns
- Avoid mixing this blue with warm reds — creates visual discord
- Never use blue on blue text (e.g., body text on tinted backgrounds)
- Avoid comic or rounded display fonts — undermines professional intent
- No photographic backgrounds that compete with blue palette

## Style 03: Professional Gray

### Overview
Professional Gray uses the restrained elegance of neutral grays with a single controlled blue accent to signal competence without asserting dominance. This style works universally across sectors and is the go-to when the audience, not the presenter, is the focus. Ideal for data-dense presentations.

### Color Palette
- Primary: `#2D3748` (Charcoal)
- Secondary: `#4A5568` (Dark Gray)
- Accent: `#3182CE` (Signal Blue)
- Background: `#FFFFFF` (White)
- Text Primary: `#1A202C` (Near Black)
- Text Secondary: `#718096` (Medium Gray)
- Border: `#E2E8F0` (Pale Gray)

### Typography
- Heading: Inter, Bold, 34–42pt — neutral and systematic
- Body: Inter, Regular, 15–17pt — designed for screens
- Caption: Inconsolata, Regular, 10pt — for technical labels and citations

### Layout Principles
- Thin 2pt charcoal rule under page title (not decorative — structural only)
- Gray gradient sidebar (subtle, 5% opacity) on left edge for visual anchoring
- Tables and charts use alternating `#F7FAFC` rows with `#E2E8F0` borders
- White space ratio: at least 35% of slide surface unoccupied
- Footer line in pale gray with page number and section name

### When to Use
- Data analytics and business intelligence presentations
- Internal reporting where design should not distract from numbers
- HR and operations decks requiring neutrality across political sensitivities
- Consulting deliverables where client brand takes priority

### Anti-Patterns
- Avoid warm brown or red tones — breaks the cool neutral palette
- Never use gray text on gray backgrounds (contrast failure)
- Avoid decorative motifs or illustrated icons — keep it utilitarian
- No color-coded elements without a legend; grays are ambiguous

## Style 04: Enterprise Dark

### Overview
Enterprise Dark brings the visual weight and authority of dark-mode interfaces into formal presentation contexts. Deep slate backgrounds with cool blue accents signal cutting-edge sophistication — appropriate for technology companies and innovation divisions presenting to executive audiences who equate dark mode with premium quality.

### Color Palette
- Primary: `#0EA5E9` (Sky Blue Accent)
- Secondary: `#38BDF8` (Light Cyan)
- Accent: `#F59E0B` (Amber)
- Background: `#0F172A` (Deep Slate)
- Text Primary: `#F1F5F9` (Near White)
- Text Secondary: `#94A3B8` (Muted Blue-Gray)
- Border: `#1E293B` (Dark Border)

### Typography
- Heading: Inter, ExtraBold, 36–44pt — demands attention on dark
- Body: Inter, Regular, 15–17pt — high contrast against dark
- Caption: IBM Plex Mono, Regular, 10pt — for technical annotations

### Layout Principles
- Slide background is solid dark slate — no gradients for enterprise formality
- Accent color used exclusively on key numbers, CTAs, and primary action items
- Card elements use slightly lighter `#1E293B` with 1pt border — dark-on-dark depth
- Icon set must be filled (not outline) for legibility on dark surfaces
- Text contrast ratio must exceed 7:1 for all body text

### When to Use
- Technology product presentations to technical enterprise audiences
- Innovation lab showcases and R&D briefings
- Executive dashboards embedded in dark-mode reporting tools
- Evening and evening venue presentations (dark environment)

### Anti-Patterns
- Avoid pure black `#000000` background — too harsh and not modern
- Never use low-contrast text (light gray on near-white) — critical accessibility failure
- Avoid warm color accents (orange-red) — creates jarring contrast with cool palette
- No printed handout derivation — dark backgrounds consume ink

## Style 05: Business Formal

### Overview
Business Formal is the crisp, unambiguous presentation style of law firms, financial institutions, and professional services. Black and white with a single restrained accent communicates seriousness and precision. Every element must justify its existence; this style has zero tolerance for visual noise.

### Color Palette
- Primary: `#000000` (Black)
- Secondary: `#1A1A1A` (Off-Black)
- Accent: `#B91C1C` (Deep Red)
- Background: `#FFFFFF` (White)
- Text Primary: `#111111` (Near Black)
- Text Secondary: `#555555` (Dark Gray)
- Border: `#CCCCCC` (Light Gray)

### Typography
- Heading: Georgia, Bold, 32–40pt — classic serif authority
- Body: Georgia, Regular, 15–17pt — consistent serif throughout
- Caption: Courier New, Regular, 9–10pt, spaced — formal footnote register

### Layout Principles
- Top header bar: thin 2pt black rule below slide title (structural, not decorative)
- Single accent touch allowed per slide: one red element maximum
- Content uses single-column layout with formal paragraph spacing
- Tables formatted with visible borders — gridded, not striped
- Slide numbers in lower-right corner, consistent throughout

### When to Use
- Legal, financial, and regulatory presentations
- Annual general meetings and compliance briefings
- Academic defense presentations requiring gravitas
- Formal proposals to conservative institutional clients

### Anti-Patterns
- Avoid any casual fonts — no rounded or hand-drawn typefaces
- Never use more than one accent color element per slide
- Avoid photography or illustration — this style is text and data only
- No animations or transitions — still and static only

## Style 06: Clean White

### Overview
Clean White pursues the ideal of radical reduction — every element present is essential, every element absent is intentional. Inspired by product design presentations from hardware and software companies that let their work speak. The single accent color is deployed like a spotlight: one point of emphasis per slide.

### Color Palette
- Primary: `#0071E3` (Apple-ish Blue)
- Secondary: `#1D1D1F` (Near Black)
- Accent: `#0071E3` (Focus Blue)
- Background: `#FFFFFF` (White)
- Text Primary: `#1D1D1F` (Near Black)
- Text Secondary: `#6E6E73` (Muted Gray)
- Border: `#F5F5F7` (Barely Gray)

### Typography
- Heading: SF Pro Display (or system-ui), Bold, 40–52pt — San Francisco system elegance
- Body: SF Pro Text (or system-ui), Regular, 16–18pt — engineered for readability
- Caption: SF Pro Text, Light, 11pt — restrained metadata voice

### Layout Principles
- 80pt margins on all sides — generous breathing room is the style
- One key statement per slide; resist the urge to fill space
- Images (if used) are edge-to-edge or floating with 32pt clearance
- Accent color used on a single word, number, or icon per slide
- No decorative separators — whitespace IS the separator

### When to Use
- Technology product launches and feature announcements
- Design system and UX review presentations
- Portfolio showcases where content quality must dominate
- Keynote-style narrative presentations to sophisticated audiences

### Anti-Patterns
- Avoid multiple focal points — one emphasis point only
- Never fill whitespace with more content; whitespace is the design
- Avoid gradients, textures, or backgrounds with visual noise
- No bullet-point lists — convert to individual statement slides instead

## Style 07: Nordic Minimal

### Overview
Nordic Minimal channels Scandinavian design philosophy: warmth through restraint, richness through texture, meaning through absence. The warm cream-and-sage palette feels handcrafted and considered. This style excels when the brand needs to feel human and intentional rather than corporate and automated.

### Color Palette
- Primary: `#3D5247` (Forest Sage)
- Secondary: `#6B8E7F` (Muted Sage)
- Accent: `#C17F5B` (Warm Clay)
- Background: `#F4F0EA` (Warm Cream)
- Text Primary: `#2A2825` (Warm Near-Black)
- Text Secondary: `#7A6E65` (Warm Gray)
- Border: `#DDD4C8` (Warm Border)

### Typography
- Heading: DM Serif Display, Regular, 38–48pt — editorial warmth
- Body: Lato, Light, 15–17pt — approachable and clean
- Caption: Space Mono, Regular, 9pt, 4pt letter-spacing — artisanal contrast

### Layout Principles
- Warm cream background throughout — never pure white
- Single organic blob shape as textural background element (10% opacity)
- Title and key text left-aligned with 60pt left margin
- One botanical or geometric accent mark in clay tone
- Footer: thin warm gray rule + caption in wide-spaced monospace

### When to Use
- Wellness, lifestyle, and sustainability brands
- Non-profit and social enterprise communications
- Creative agency capabilities and philosophy decks
- Food, travel, and cultural sector presentations

### Anti-Patterns
- Avoid digital-looking blues or teals — they break the organic warmth
- Never use harsh black text; always use warm near-black `#2A2825`
- Avoid symmetrical rigid grids — asymmetry adds handcraft feel
- No stock photography with corporate staging — use authentic imagery

## Style 08: Swiss Grid

### Overview
Swiss Grid is the direct descendant of International Typographic Style: strict column grids, Helvetica, and the signal-red accent of the Swiss design tradition. Functional to the point of severity, this style communicates authority through order itself. Information architecture IS the aesthetic.

### Color Palette
- Primary: `#E8000D` (Signal Red)
- Secondary: `#111111` (Near Black)
- Accent: `#E8000D` (Signal Red)
- Background: `#FFFFFF` (White)
- Text Primary: `#111111` (Near Black)
- Text Secondary: `#444444` (Dark Gray)
- Border: `#DDDDDD` (Rule Gray)

### Typography
- Heading: Helvetica Neue Bold (or Arial Bold), 32–44pt, tight leading (0.9×)
- Body: Helvetica Neue Regular (or Arial), 12–14pt — the typographic baseline
- Caption: Helvetica Neue Light, 9pt, 4–6pt letter-spacing, uppercase

### Layout Principles
- 12-column grid enforced; every element snaps to column boundaries
- Vertical 6pt red rule on left edge as structural anchor
- Single horizontal divider rule between title zone and content zone
- Text columns maintain consistent 12pt gutters
- Numerical labels aligned to grid with monospaced precision

### When to Use
- Consulting and finance presentations requiring institutional authority
- Government and policy briefings
- Design conference presentations that reference design history
- Information-dense data narratives requiring maximum structure

### Anti-Patterns
- Avoid decorative or illustrative elements that break grid discipline
- Never use rounded corners on any shape or container
- Avoid more than two typeface weights in a single deck
- No gradients — flat color blocks only

## Style 09: Flat Modern

### Overview
Flat Modern applies the clean, icon-forward language of material design to presentation contexts. Bold flat color blocks, generous padding, and a friendly-but-structured hierarchy make complex information approachable. Best for digital product companies, startups, and teams presenting to mixed-expertise audiences.

### Color Palette
- Primary: `#6C63FF` (Modern Violet)
- Secondary: `#FF6584` (Coral Pink)
- Accent: `#43B89C` (Teal Green)
- Background: `#F5F6FA` (Near White)
- Text Primary: `#2D3436` (Charcoal)
- Text Secondary: `#636E72` (Medium Gray)
- Border: `#DFE6E9` (Pale Blue-Gray)

### Typography
- Heading: Nunito, ExtraBold, 34–42pt — friendly and bold
- Body: Nunito, Regular, 15–17pt — comfortable readability
- Caption: Nunito, Light, 10pt — soft metadata voice

### Layout Principles
- Bento-style card layouts for multi-point slides (2×2 or 3×1 grids)
- Each card has 24pt internal padding and a subtle `#DFE6E9` border
- Icon-leading bullets: 24pt flat icon + text at same baseline
- Section divider slides use full-bleed primary color with white title
- Color-coded tags for categories and priorities

### When to Use
- Startup pitch decks and product demo presentations
- Design sprints and agile retrospective presentations
- App onboarding and feature walkthrough decks
- Tech meetup and hackathon presentations

### Anti-Patterns
- Avoid gradients — flat means flat, no exceptions
- Never mix two warm accent colors in the same slide
- Avoid serif body text — this style lives in sans-serif territory
- No photorealistic illustrations — icon-forward only

## Style 10: Monochrome

### Overview
Monochrome commits to a single hue and develops its full palette through tints, shades, and tones alone. The discipline creates visual harmony that registers as premium and considered. This style rewards photographers who trust their imagery to carry chromatic interest while the typography and layout remain unified.

### Color Palette
- Primary: `#1A56DB` (Base Blue)
- Secondary: `#3A7BD5` (Medium Blue)
- Accent: `#93C5FD` (Light Blue)
- Background: `#EFF6FF` (Pale Blue)
- Text Primary: `#1E3A5F` (Deep Blue)
- Text Secondary: `#4B7BE5` (Mid Blue)
- Border: `#BFDBFE` (Very Light Blue)

### Typography
- Heading: Raleway, Bold, 36–44pt — architectural without decoration
- Body: Raleway, Regular, 15–17pt — consistent with heading family
- Caption: Raleway, Light, 10pt, 2pt letter-spacing — whisper-quiet metadata

### Layout Principles
- All elements use the same base hue at varying lightness levels only
- Background tint is no more than 10% saturation for near-neutrality
- Typography hierarchy established through size and weight, not color
- Photography (if used) converted to monochrome tint matching the palette
- Dividers use tinted borders rather than gray rules

### When to Use
- Brand identity presentations for single-color brand systems
- Portfolio reviews where work contains diverse colors
- Academic research where controlled palette reduces distraction
- Elegant corporate anniversary or milestone presentations

### Anti-Patterns
- Avoid introducing any out-of-family hue — breaks monochrome discipline
- Never use pure white or pure black within the monochrome system
- Avoid low-saturation backgrounds that cause text to disappear
- No warm undertones mixed into cool monochromes (or vice versa)

## Style 11: Vibrant Pop

### Overview
Vibrant Pop channels the kinetic energy of contemporary brand design: high saturation, bold color blocking, and deliberate asymmetry create slides that feel alive. Designed for audiences under 40, product launches, and cultural moments where being memorable matters more than being safe.

### Color Palette
- Primary: `#FF3B5C` (Hot Coral)
- Secondary: `#FFD700` (Electric Yellow)
- Accent: `#00E5FF` (Cyan)
- Background: `#FFFFFF` (White)
- Text Primary: `#1A1A1A` (Near Black)
- Text Secondary: `#555555` (Dark Gray)
- Border: `#F0F0F0` (Pale Border)

### Typography
- Heading: Barlow Condensed, ExtraBold, 48–64pt — punchy and wide
- Body: Barlow, Regular, 15–17pt — readable companion
- Caption: Barlow, Light Italic, 10pt — relaxed metadata

### Layout Principles
- Color blocks occupy 30–50% of slide as background shapes (not full bleed)
- Asymmetric composition: title top-left, color block bottom-right
- Bold horizontal stripes of primary color as section dividers
- No more than 3 colors visible in a single slide
- Large typographic numbers (60pt+) as data callouts in color blocks

### When to Use
- Consumer product and lifestyle brand presentations
- Fashion, entertainment, and music industry decks
- Youth-oriented marketing campaigns and trend reports
- Creative agency new business pitches

### Anti-Patterns
- Avoid pairing all three accent colors simultaneously — choose two per slide
- Never use this style for financial, legal, or medical presentations
- Avoid serif fonts — clashes with the contemporary energy
- No muted or dusty tones mixed into the vibrant palette

## Style 12: Bold Contrast

### Overview
Bold Contrast strips the presentation to its most essential visual opposition: light and dark, foreground and background, statement and silence. Black and one bold color create a binary rhythm that makes every slide feel like a deliberate editorial choice. Ideal for brand manifestos and high-stakes announcements.

### Color Palette
- Primary: `#FF5100` (Vivid Orange)
- Secondary: `#000000` (Black)
- Accent: `#FFFFFF` (White)
- Background: `#0A0A0A` (Near Black)
- Text Primary: `#FFFFFF` (White)
- Text Secondary: `#FF5100` (Vivid Orange)
- Border: `#1A1A1A` (Dark Border)

### Typography
- Heading: Anton, Regular, 52–72pt — compressed, loud, unmistakable
- Body: Cabin, Regular, 15–17pt — contrasting geometric body
- Caption: IBM Plex Mono, Regular, 9pt — technical counterpoint

### Layout Principles
- Alternating slide treatment: dark background with white/orange text OR orange block with dark text
- One massive typographic element per slide (50%+ of slide height)
- Color used as absolute signal — every orange element is the most important thing
- Zero decorative elements — contrast IS the decoration
- Slide transitions create visual rhythm: alternating light and dark

### When to Use
- Product launch events and keynote-style announcements
- Brand campaign kickoffs and internal rallying presentations
- Conference opening slides and section breaks
- Award shows and recognition event presentations

### Anti-Patterns
- Avoid medium tones — commit to extremes only
- Never use orange as a secondary/subtle color; always let it dominate or disappear
- Avoid complex charts — this style is for statements, not data analysis
- No imagery that competes with the typography

## Style 13: Electric Neon

### Overview
Electric Neon creates the visual language of advanced technology interfaces: dark environments, glowing accent colors, and monospace typography that signals system-level intelligence. The aesthetic borrows from terminal interfaces, HUD displays, and sci-fi motion graphics — communicating innovation through its own visual grammar.

### Color Palette
- Primary: `#00FF88` (Neon Green)
- Secondary: `#7B00FF` (Electric Violet)
- Accent: `#00D4FF` (Cyan)
- Background: `#050510` (Deep Space)
- Text Primary: `#E0E0F0` (Soft White)
- Text Secondary: `#8888BB` (Muted Lavender)
- Border: `#0A0A2A` (Dark Navy)

### Typography
- Heading: Barlow Condensed, ExtraBold, 44–60pt, 4–8pt letter-spacing
- Body: IBM Plex Mono, Regular, 13–15pt — code-adjacent authority
- Caption: IBM Plex Mono, Regular, 9pt — system annotation style

### Layout Principles
- Near-black background with large blurred glow circles (30–50pt blur radius)
- Gradient text on title: green to cyan to violet multi-stop
- Dark semi-transparent panel behind body text for legibility

### When to Use

- Cybersecurity and infrastructure technology conferences
- Gaming industry presentations and esports events

### Anti-Patterns

- Avoid serif fonts — incongruent with the technical aesthetic
- No photographic imagery on neon backgrounds — too noisy

## Style 14: Gradient Flow

### Overview
Gradient Flow expresses movement and transformation through carefully orchestrated multi-stop color transitions. Unlike simple two-color gradients, this style uses four to six color nodes distributed across the background to create painterly depth. The result feels digital-native and contemporary — used by creative agencies and design-forward technology brands.

### Color Palette
- Primary: `#FF6EC7` (Hot Pink)
- Secondary: `#7B61FF` (Violet)
- Accent: `#00D4FF` (Cyan)
- Background: `#FFB347` (Warm Amber — gradient node)
- Text Primary: `#FFFFFF` (White)
- Text Secondary: `#FFFFFF` (White, 80% opacity)
- Border: `#FFFFFF` (White, 20% opacity)

### Typography
- Heading: Bebas Neue, Regular, 52–72pt — condensed and powerful on gradients
- Body: Poppins, Light, 14–16pt — clean against complex backgrounds
- Caption: Poppins, Light, 10pt — recedes into gradient

### Layout Principles
- Full-bleed multi-point gradient as primary background (not solid colors)
- All text in white with subtle drop shadow for gradient legibility
- Frosted glass card (white 15% opacity, border 25% opacity) for body content
- Minimal text per slide — gradient requires space to breathe
- Large centered title with subtitle below; no left/right column splits

### When to Use
- Brand launches and rebranding announcements
- Creative portfolio presentations
- Music, fashion, and cultural institution communications
- Award ceremonies and gala event presentations

### Anti-Patterns
- Avoid linear two-stop gradients — they look dated; use radial multi-stop
- Never darken gradient backgrounds to compensate for legibility — use white text with shadow
- Avoid dense body copy on gradient backgrounds — contrast breaks down
- No competing color blocks; let the gradient be the only visual statement

Dynamic Energy uses diagonal compositions, angular cutoffs, and motion-suggesting shapes to communicate momentum. This style is designed for performance, sports, and high-growth contexts where the visual language must match the brand's forward velocity. Every shape leans forward; nothing is static.

### Color Palette
- Primary: `#FF1744` (Power Red)
- Secondary: `#FF6D00` (Energy Orange)
- Accent: `#FFEA00` (Electric Yellow)
- Background: `#0D0D0D` (Near Black)

- Border: `#1A1A1A` (Dark Border)

### Typography
- Heading: Barlow, Black, 42–56pt — maximum impact weight
- Body: Barlow, SemiBold, 15–17pt — maintains energy

### Anti-Patterns

- Avoid dense data tables and charts — this style is for statements, not analysis
- No serif fonts — incompatible with the kinetic aesthetic

## Style 17: Research Paper

### Overview
Research Paper translates the visual logic of academic publishing directly to slides: structured hierarchy, systematic use of space, and a neutral color palette that never competes with data. This style prioritizes information transmission over visual interest — the data is the content; the design is the frame.

### Color Palette
- Primary: `#1A4480` (Research Blue)
- Secondary: `#2E75B6` (Figure Blue)
- Accent: `#C00000` (Highlight Red)
- Background: `#FFFFFF` (White)
- Text Primary: `#000000` (Black)
- Text Secondary: `#595959` (Dark Gray)
- Border: `#D0D0D0` (Rule Gray)

### Typography
- Heading: Libre Baskerville, Bold, 28–36pt — academic serif standard
- Body: Libre Baskerville, Regular, 14–16pt — consistent with heading
- Caption: Courier New, Regular, 9pt — citation and figure label register

### Layout Principles
- Two-column grid for text-heavy slides (57%/43% split)
- Figure captions always below figure, left-aligned, with label prefix (Fig. 1)
- Tables with 1pt gridlines and light `#F5F5F5` header row
- Equation blocks centered with numbering right-aligned
- Section numbering visible throughout (1. Introduction, 2. Methods)

### When to Use
- Academic conference presentations (ICML, NeurIPS, AGU, EGU style)
- Research lab group meetings and internal scientific reviews
- Grant proposal presentations to scientific panels
- Data-heavy analysis presentations for technical stakeholders

### Anti-Patterns
- Avoid color for decoration — use color only to encode information
- Never use decorative icons where plain text would suffice
- Avoid animations beyond simple slide transitions
- No typographic experiments — legibility is the only goal

## Style 18: Scientific Data

### Overview
Scientific Data is engineered around data visualization: the palette is colorblind-accessible, the layouts prioritize chart area, and the typography retreats to let the data speak. Designed for presentations where the chart IS the argument, not an illustration of it.

### Color Palette
- Primary: `#0072B2` (Okabe-Ito Blue)
- Secondary: `#E69F00` (Okabe-Ito Orange)
- Accent: `#009E73` (Okabe-Ito Green)
