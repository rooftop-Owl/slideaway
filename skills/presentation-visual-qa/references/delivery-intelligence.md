# Delivery Intelligence Reference

**Scope**: How to design slide content that supports effective delivery. Covers opening slide strategies, closing slide design, transition slide patterns, speaker notes templates, Q&A slide design, and virtual meeting adaptations.

**Does NOT cover**: Human coaching elements — body language, posture, speaking pace, vocal delivery, or audience rapport techniques. This file is strictly about SLIDE DESIGN that supports delivery.

---

## 1. Opening Slide Strategies

The first slide sets the frame for everything that follows. Six proven opening types — each defined by what the SLIDE looks like, not how you perform it.

### 1.1 Surprising Statistic

**What the slide looks like:**
- Single large number dominates the layout (72pt+, bold, center or left-anchored)
- One-line context label below the number in 24–28pt
- Source attribution in 14pt bottom-right corner
- Minimal or no background imagery — the number IS the visual
- No bullet points, no title header competing for attention

**Example layout:**
```
[blank space]

        83%

of coastal infrastructure will be
at risk by 2050 under SSP5-8.5

                              Source: IPCC AR6, 2021
```

**Speaker notes template:**
> "That number stopped me when I first saw it. Here's what it means for the work we're about to discuss..."

---

### 1.2 Provocative Question

**What the slide looks like:**
- Question text in 36–44pt, sentence case, centered vertically and horizontally
- No answer on the slide — the question stands alone
- Optional: subtle visual metaphor in background at 10–15% opacity
- No bullet points, no sub-questions, no hedging text
- Slide title field left blank or set to the question itself

**Example layout:**
```
[centered vertically]

  What if the model we've trusted
  for 20 years is systematically wrong?
```

**Speaker notes template:**
> "I'm going to answer this by the end of the talk. But first, let me show you why it matters..."

---

### 1.3 Data Teaser

**What the slide looks like:**
- A partially revealed chart or map — key data obscured by a shape, blur, or "?" overlay
- Title: "You'll see this again" or left blank
- Teaser label: "Coming up: [topic]" in small text
- Creates visual curiosity without front-loading the conclusion
- Typically 60–70% of the figure is visible; the critical region is hidden

**Example layout:**
```
  [Map with key hotspot region blurred]

  Where is the highest-risk zone?
  → We'll return to this in 12 minutes.
```

**Speaker notes template:**
> "I'm deliberately not showing you the full picture yet. By the time we get back to this, you'll have the context to read it properly..."

---

### 1.4 Knowledge Gap

**What the slide looks like:**
- Two-column layout: "What we know" (left) vs "What we don't know" (right)
- Left column: 3–4 bullet points, green checkmarks or solid dots
- Right column: 3–4 bullet points, red X marks or open circles — the gaps
- The gap column is visually heavier (bolder font, more items, or highlighted)
- Implicit message: this talk fills the right column

**Example layout:**
```
  What we know              What we don't know
  ─────────────             ──────────────────
  ✓ Hazard frequency        ✗ Compound event timing
  ✓ Asset exposure          ✗ Adaptive capacity decay
  ✓ Historical losses       ✗ Tipping point thresholds
```

**Speaker notes template:**
> "The left column is well-covered in the literature. The right column is where this work lives..."

---

### 1.5 Scope and Scale

**What the slide looks like:**
- A map, timeline, or grid that visually communicates the scale of the problem domain
- Annotations marking key boundaries: geographic extent, time horizon, number of entities
- Color-coded regions or time periods to show coverage
- No dense text — the visual IS the message
- Optional: a single summary stat overlaid ("47 countries · 2020–2100 · 3 SSP scenarios")

**Example layout:**
```
  [World map with study regions highlighted]

  47 countries  ·  80-year horizon  ·  3 climate scenarios
```

**Speaker notes template:**
> "Before I show you any results, I want you to understand the scope of what we analyzed..."

---

### 1.6 Before/After Contrast

**What the slide looks like:**
- Split layout: left half labeled "Before" or "Current State", right half "After" or "Future State"
- Visual contrast is stark — different colors, different densities, different scales
- Minimal text; the visual difference carries the argument
- Optional arrow or dividing line between the two states
- Works best with maps, charts, or photographs (not text lists)

**Example layout:**
```
  2020 Risk Profile          2080 Risk Profile
  ─────────────────          ─────────────────
  [light blue map]           [deep red map]

         ←──── 60 years of climate change ────→
```

**Speaker notes template:**
> "This is where we are. This is where we're headed. Everything in between is what this research explains..."

## 2. Closing Slide Design

The closing slide is the last thing the audience sees. It must work as a standalone artifact — someone photographing it should capture the essential message.

### 2.1 Key Takeaways

**What the slide looks like:**
- Title: "Key Takeaways" or "What to Remember"
- 3 bullet points maximum — each a complete, standalone sentence
- Each bullet starts with a strong verb or number
- 24–28pt font — larger than body slides
- Optional: numbered list (1, 2, 3) to signal priority order

**Example:**
```
  Key Takeaways

  1. Compound events increase AAI by 2.3× under SSP5-8.5
  2. Coastal adaptation reduces losses by up to 40% if deployed by 2040
  3. Current risk models underestimate tail losses by a factor of 3
```

**Speaker notes template:**
> "Three things I want you to walk out with. [Read each one.] If you remember nothing else from today, remember number two — because that's the actionable one..."

---

### 2.2 Call-Back

**What the slide looks like:**
- Returns to the opening slide's visual or question — same layout, same image, same number
- Now annotated with the answer or resolution
- Creates visual closure: "We started here, now we know..."
- Often uses a before/after annotation overlay on the original graphic
- The repetition signals intentional structure, not laziness

**Example:**
> Opening slide showed a blurred map. Closing slide shows the same map, fully revealed, with the answer labeled.

**Speaker notes template:**
> "Remember this map from the first slide? Now you have the context to read it. The answer to my opening question is [X]..."

---

### 2.3 Implications (So What → Now What)

**What the slide looks like:**
- Two-section layout: "So What?" (top half) and "Now What?" (bottom half)
- Dividing line or color band between sections
- "So What" = 2–3 implications of the findings
- "Now What" = 2–3 concrete next steps or recommendations
- Action-oriented language throughout

**Example:**
```
  So What?
  • Current infrastructure investment is systematically underpriced
  • Regulatory frameworks lag observed risk by 15–20 years

  ─────────────────────────────────────────────

  Now What?
  • Update national risk registers using compound event models
  • Pilot adaptive investment frameworks in 3 high-exposure regions
```

**Speaker notes template:**
> "The findings are clear. Here's what they mean for practice, and here's what I'd recommend doing about it..."

---

### 2.4 Visual Summary

**What the slide looks like:**
- A single figure that synthesizes the entire narrative — often the most important chart in the talk
- Annotated with 3–5 callout labels pointing to key features
- Title: "The Full Picture" or the paper's main finding as a sentence
- No bullet points — the figure does the work
- High resolution, full-bleed or near-full-bleed layout

**Speaker notes template:**
> "This one figure contains everything I've shown you today. [Walk through each annotation.] This is the slide I'd want you to share with colleagues who weren't here..."

---

### 2.5 Future Outlook

**What the slide looks like:**
- Timeline or roadmap visual showing next steps, open questions, or planned work
- 3–5 milestones or research questions labeled on the timeline
- Optional: "Where we are now" marker on the timeline
- Forward-looking language: "Next," "Planned," "Open question"
- Signals ongoing work without overselling incomplete results

**Example:**
```
  ●────────────────────────────────────────→
  Now      2026         2027          2028+
  │        │            │             │
  This     Validation   Multi-hazard  Policy
  work     study        extension     uptake
```

**Speaker notes template:**
> "This work opens more questions than it closes. Here's where we're headed next, and where collaboration opportunities exist..."

## 3. Transition Slide Templates

Transition slides signal structure. They reset audience attention and prevent the "where are we?" confusion that kills comprehension.

### 3.1 Section Divider Design

**What the slide looks like:**
- Full-bleed background color (from the presentation's accent palette)
- Section number + section title in large white text (40–48pt)
- Optional: brief one-line description of what this section covers (20pt)
- No figures, no bullets, no data
- High contrast: dark background + white text, or vice versa

**Template:**
```
  [Full dark blue background]

  02

  Methods

  How we quantified compound climate risk
```

**Design rule**: Section number should be visually larger than the section title. The number anchors position in the talk structure.

---

### 3.2 Bridge Slide Pattern

**What the slide looks like:**
- Two-panel layout: "What we just established" (left) + "What comes next" (right)
- Left panel: 1–2 bullet summary of the preceding section
- Right panel: 1–2 bullet preview of the next section
- Arrow or visual connector between panels
- Subdued color treatment — this is a navigation slide, not a content slide

**Template:**
```
  We established...              Now we'll examine...
  ─────────────────              ────────────────────
  • Hazard frequency             • How exposure
    increases 2× under             amplifies these
    SSP5-8.5                       hazards
  • Coastal regions              • Which asset classes
    most affected                  are most vulnerable
                    ────→
```

---

### 3.3 Verbal Bridge Speaker Notes

For any transition slide, the speaker notes should follow this pattern:

```
[Summary of what was just covered — 1 sentence]
[Explicit connection to what comes next — 1 sentence]
[Preview of the key question the next section answers — 1 sentence]
```

**Example:**
> "We've now established that hazard frequency is increasing. The question is whether our infrastructure exposure is keeping pace with that risk — that's what the next section addresses. Specifically: are we building in the right places?"

## 4. Speaker Notes Templates Per Slide Type

Consistent speaker notes structure reduces cognitive load during delivery and ensures key points aren't dropped.

### 4.1 Title Slide Notes

```
[Opening hook — the one sentence that frames why this matters]
[Credentials signal — why you are the right person to present this]
[Roadmap — "In the next X minutes, I'll cover A, B, and C"]
[Time check — "We'll have Y minutes for questions at the end"]
```

---

### 4.2 Methods Slide Notes

```
[One-sentence summary of the approach]
[Why this method over alternatives — the key design choice]
[Known limitations — name them before the audience does]
[Transition: "With that setup, here's what we found..."]
```

---

### 4.3 Results Slide Notes

```
[What the figure shows — describe it before interpreting it]
[The key finding — the single most important number or pattern]
[Comparison or context — "This is X times larger than..."]
[Uncertainty — "The error bars reflect..."]
[Transition: "This leads to the question of..."]
```

---

### 4.4 Discussion Slide Notes

```
[Restate the main finding in plain language]
[Mechanism — why does this happen?]
[Implications — what does this mean for practice or policy?]
[Limitations — what can't we conclude from this?]
[Transition: "Let me now summarize..."]
```

---

### 4.5 Q&A Invitation Notes

```
[Signal the end: "That brings me to the end of the prepared talk"]
[Restate the core message: "The main takeaway is [X]"]
[Open the floor: "I'm happy to take questions — particularly on [topic]"]
[Backup slide reminder: "I have backup slides on [A], [B], [C] if useful"]
```

## 5. Q&A Slide Design

The Q&A slide must function as a holding slide — visible for potentially 10–20 minutes while discussion happens.

### 5.1 Holding Slide Content

**Required elements:**
- Title: "Questions" or "Discussion" (large, 40pt+)
- Contact information: email address in readable font (20pt minimum)
- QR code: links to paper, dataset, or project page (minimum 2cm × 2cm)
- Key figure: the single most important result from the talk, small but readable

**Layout template:**
```
  Questions

  [Key figure — right side, 40% of slide width]

  jane.doe@institution.edu

  [QR code]          Paper: doi.org/10.xxxx/xxxxx
  Scan for data      Slides: bit.ly/talk-shortlink
```

**Design rationale**: The key figure gives the audience something to look at and discuss. The QR code enables immediate follow-up. Contact info removes the friction of "how do I reach you?"

---

### 5.2 Backup Slide Organization

Backup slides should be organized by anticipated question category, not by presentation order.

**Recommended categories:**
1. **Methodology details** — deeper dives on model choices, validation, sensitivity
2. **Data sources** — provenance, quality, known issues
3. **Alternative scenarios** — results under different assumptions
4. **Comparison to prior work** — how results relate to existing literature
5. **Policy implications** — practical applications not covered in main talk

**Labeling convention:**
```
  [BACKUP: Methodology] Slide B1
  [BACKUP: Data] Slide B2
  [BACKUP: Scenarios] Slide B3
```

**Navigation tip in notes:**
> "If asked about [X], go to backup slide B2. If asked about [Y], go to B4."

## 6. Virtual Meeting Adaptations

Slide design for virtual delivery requires specific adjustments. Screen rendering, compression artifacts, and divided attention change what works.

### 6.1 Font Size Adjustments

- **Body text**: Add +4pt to all body text sizes
  - Standard body: 20pt → Virtual body: 24pt
  - Caption text: 14pt → Virtual caption: 18pt
  - Footnotes: 12pt → Virtual footnotes: 16pt
- **Rationale**: Screen sharing compresses visual fidelity; smaller text becomes illegible at typical screen-share resolutions

### 6.2 Content Density Reduction

- **Target**: -20% content density vs. in-person slides
- **In practice**: If a slide has 5 bullet points, reduce to 4. If a figure has 6 labeled annotations, reduce to 5.
- **Rationale**: Virtual audiences have divided attention (chat, notifications, other windows). Less content per slide = more retention per slide.

### 6.3 Contrast Requirements

- **Minimum**: AA standard (4.5:1 contrast ratio for normal text)
- **Target for virtual**: AAA standard (7:1 contrast ratio)
- **Check with**: WebAIM Contrast Checker or the `check_contrast()` function in `visual-qa-automation.md`
- **Common failure**: Light gray text on white background — fails at any standard
- **Safe combinations**: Black on white (21:1), white on dark navy (15:1+), white on dark green (8:1+)

### 6.4 Animation Restrictions

- **Avoid**: Complex entrance animations, motion paths, 3D transitions
- **Permitted**: Simple fade-in (0.3s), appear on click
- **Rationale**: Screen-share frame rates (typically 15–30fps) make complex animations appear choppy and distract from content
- **Alternative**: Use progressive disclosure via separate slides rather than animation

### 6.5 Explicit Slide Numbers

- **Requirement**: Every slide must show a slide number (bottom-right or bottom-center)
- **Format**: "7 / 24" preferred over "7" alone — gives audience orientation
- **Rationale**: Virtual audiences cannot see the presenter's physical position in a slide deck. Explicit numbers enable "go back to slide 12" requests during Q&A.

### 6.6 Virtual Checklist

Before any virtual presentation, verify:

- [ ] Font sizes ≥24pt for body text
- [ ] Content density reduced by ~20% from in-person version
- [ ] All text passes AAA 7:1 contrast ratio
- [ ] No complex animations (fade-in only)
- [ ] Slide numbers visible on every slide
- [ ] QR codes tested at screen-share resolution (scan from a phone while sharing)
- [ ] Key figures have sufficient whitespace to remain legible when compressed
